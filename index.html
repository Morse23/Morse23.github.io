Analyzing the Meta of the One Piece Trading Card Game.

Analyzing the meta of One Piece's third set, Pillars of Strength, across the English and Japanese formats. In this context, meta will be defined as the top performing strategies and decks. Essentially, the most efficient decks and cards available at the time.

What's in a deck?

A standard deck consists of 50 cards, 1 leader, and 10 don cards. The cards allowed in each deck is dependent on the color of the leader. Don cards act as the 'energy' of the game, allowing you to pay the costs to play cards. As they are standard in every deck, they are not factored into any deck statistics.

What's in a leader?

Each leader has a color, a special ability unique to them, and a set life total. Their color marks the color that is allowed in the deck.

What's in a color?

In the context of the game, think of color as an archetype or a class. Each has a specific niche it fills. For example, Yellow usually involves life manipulation, and Black involves character removal.

Some more information!

The goal of the game is to bring your opponent to 0 life through attacks and effects, then successfully attack them when they have 0 life remaining. (Think of the life cards as the armor, 0 life is when you have no armor left) You can do this using characters, as well as your own leader. Usually, the game is played in a one-on-one, best of one format.

Total Color Spread

For this figure, I wanted to show the total amount of each color played across both formats. I landed on a color-coded bubble chart since all I wanted to display was simple counts.

This figure clearly shows the dominance of Red, having more representation than most of the other colors combined. A little bit of extra information: Red has been one of the most consistent/strongest colors in the game for the first few sets (expansions that release new cards), to the point where they even had to restrict certain red cards. Even after this restriction, it still was quite strong. It is also worth noting the prevalence of Yellow, the newest color in the game.

Total Leader Spread

For this, I wanted to show the amount of each leader played across both formats. The leaders are also color-coded, so inter-color rankings can also be viewed through this figure.

Across all colors, it can be seen that Whitebeard was the most represented deck, with Zoro falling close behind. Even among the other colors, they dwarf the highest representation from each by a significant amount. Another deck of note is Red/Green Law, seeing high rates of play.

English vs. Japanese Color Spread

This figure displays the comparison between the color spreads of each format. In my raw data, the Japanese format had ~100 more entries than English, so I chose to use percentages to compare their spreads to show a more honest picture

As expected, Red was the highest in both formats. Surprisingly, Red/Green has almost a 10% lead in the English format, one of the largest differences across the data.

English vs. Japanese Deck Spread

This figure displays the comparison between the leader spreads of each format. I chose this graph because it helps highlight how much more certain leaders are played than others. (If you are having trouble reading the graph, you can view it in fullscreen by clicking the rightmost icon on the graph!)

Whitebeard is played at a similar rate in both formats, while Ace has almost a 10% lead in the Japanese format than in the English. This graph also reveals that, while the rate is still relatively low, the Japanese format saw a more diverse grouping of decks than the English format.
